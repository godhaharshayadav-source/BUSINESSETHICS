Product as a Service

Customers pay for the functionality of a product, without the responsibility of repairing, replacing or disposing it.

Category: Circular Economy

+Positive Impact

This model eliminates the large capital expense of buying a specialized vehicle. It reduces waste, extends vehicle life (fleet servicing), and provides inclusive, high-end mobility as a right, not a privilege. It aligns with our $399/month subscription, covering the vehicle, maintenance, and insurance, which our simulation showed saved Sarah **$52/week**.

-Challenges

A service fleet requires high capital and complex logistics (maintenance, charging). Customers become 100% reliant on ARIA's stability. SBMC analysis also warns of a **rebound effect**: if trips are too cheap, it could incentivize excessive use unless pricing is controlled.

★ARIA Example

Sarah pays $399/month, cancelling all her old expenses on car payments, fuel, and taxis. She is subscribing to mobility and peace of mind, not owning a car. This is the core of the business model.

Rent Instead of Buy

Customers rent the product, reducing the capital typically needed to access it. Focuses on leasing assets instead of ownership.

Category: Circular Economy

+Positive Impact

This model is key to accessibility. Sarah rents the mobility she needs on-demand, rather than spending $65k-$97k on a converted van. It removes the single biggest barrier to inclusive transport. Like "Rent the Runway," ARIA pools high-value assets (the cars) so a single product is shared by many consumers.

-Challenges

The user has no backup asset; if the ARIA fleet is offline or high-demand, the customer has no mobility. This total reliance demands near-perfect service availability. It also removes the feeling of "ownership" or deep personalization (though ARIA's app aims to counter this).

★ARIA Example

Every ARIA journey is a rental. Sarah does not hire a personal driver or buy a van. She calls an ARIA when needed, renting the vehicle, the autonomous driver, and the service ecosystem for that specific trip, all covered by her subscription.

Behavior Change

Provoking customers to adopt new behaviours, reduce consumption, or change daily habits via nudges, apps, or incentives.

Category: IoT, Nudging, Quantified Self

+Positive Impact

ARIA's app acts as a "nudge." It reminds users of school pick-ups or doctor visits, encouraging them to plan. It can also incentivize greener choices, like bundling errands into one trip or sharing a ride. By being an EV, it indirectly shifts users to cleaner transport habits.

-Challenges

Nudges can feel pressuring or be mistrusted. There's a risk of unintended consequences (e.g., making travel too easy could increase congestion). There are also ethical concerns about privacy and how much ARIA should influence a user's decisions based on their health or location data.

★ARIA Example

The "Integrated Daily Errand Support" is a behavior change tool. It reminds Sarah of groceries and schedules pickups. This shifts her behavior from last-minute, high-stress trips to a more structured, less stressful, pre-planned routine.

Industrial Symbiosis

Resource sharing between industries, where one company's service becomes another's input, creating an ecosystem.

Category: Circular Economy, Sharing Economy

+Positive Impact

This is our "ecosystem moat." ARIA shares logistics with supermarkets, pharmacies, and hospitals. Retailers get an exclusive delivery service, healthcare providers get faster patient transport, and TATA utilizes its fleet efficiently. This creates high barriers to entry for rivals.

-Challenges

Deep integration creates dependency. If a key partner (e.g., a hospital) drops out, the network is fragile. It also requires complex coordination of data and legal agreements. Data privacy is a concern, as third parties get access to user trip/health info.

★ARIA Example

Our partnership with pharmacies for prescription delivery is a perfect example. ARIA's AV fleet (a logistics resource) is shared with the pharmacy (a retail resource) to create a new, efficient service that benefits both companies and, most importantly, the customer (Sarah).

Our Core Value Proposition

"Designed With, Not For."

Our value is based on co-creation and empathy. By involving stakeholders like Sarah from day one, we address *actual* pain points, not assumed ones.

The inbuilt ramp allowing Sarah to sit **next to** her child isn't a feature—it's a design choice that provides dignity, comfort, and social connection.

ARIA maintains user agency. Sarah is not controlled by her circumstances; she is given the control. This aligns with disability ethics, offering users control and voice, and affirming human dignity.
